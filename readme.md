# Alignment-Free Phylogenomics Using Protein Language Model Embeddings

## Overview

Phylogenomic analyses traditionally rely on multiple sequence alignment and evolutionary models to infer relationships between genes and species. Recent protein language models, however, learn rich sequence representations directly from large-scale protein databases and may encode evolutionary information without requiring explicit alignment.

This project investigates whether embeddings generated by the ESM-2 protein language model can recover biologically meaningful structure from protein sequences in an alignment-free setting. Using orthologous proteins sampled from OrthoDB, I compare learned ESM-2 embeddings against a classical alignment-free baseline based on k-mer composition and evaluate their ability to recover gene family structure through visualization, clustering, classification, and outlier detection.

---

## Dataset

Protein sequences were obtained from OrthoDB and grouped into four conserved gene families spanning diverse eukaryotic taxa:

- HSP70 (Heat Shock Protein 70)
- RPS3 (Ribosomal Protein S3)
- GAPDH (Glyceraldehyde-3-Phosphate Dehydrogenase)
- Cytochrome C

The final dataset contained:

| Family       | Sequences |
| ------------ | --------: |
| HSP70        |        80 |
| RPS3         |        80 |
| GAPDH        |        80 |
| Cytochrome C |        80 |
| **Total**    |   **320** |

Headers were reformatted to preserve family labels in the form:

```text
>HSP70|Homo_sapiens
>RPS3|Arabidopsis_thaliana
```

These labels were used as ground truth throughout the analysis.

## Methods

### Protein Language Model Embeddings

Protein representations were generated using:

- Model: **ESM-2 (esm2_t6_8M_UR50D)**
- Embedding dimension: **320**
- Pooling strategy: mean pooling of final-layer token representations

Each sequence was converted into a fixed-length embedding vector suitable for downstream machine learning analyses.

### Dimensionality Reduction

To visualize embedding structure, UMAP was applied with:

- n_neighbors = 15
- min_dist = 0.1
- metric = cosine

### Classification

A multilayer perceptron (MLP) classifier was trained to predict protein family labels from ESM-2 embeddings:

- Hidden layers: (256, 128)
- Train/test split: 80/20 stratified
- Baseline: majority-class dummy classifier

### Embedding Outlier Detection

KMeans clustering (k = 2 and k = 3) was applied separately within each protein family. Sequences assigned to minority clusters representing fewer than 20% of family members were flagged as embedding outliers.

### Alignment-Free Baseline

As a classical baseline, each protein was represented by normalized 3-mer amino acid frequencies.

- k = 3
- 20 standard amino acids
- cosine distance
- agglomerative clustering

This baseline captures local sequence composition but contains no learned biological information.

---

## Results

### Embedding-Based Classification

| Model          |  Accuracy |
| -------------- | --------: |
| Dummy Baseline |     25.0% |
| ESM-2 + MLP    | **79.7%** |

The ESM-2 classifier substantially outperformed the baseline, indicating that protein family identity is strongly encoded within the learned embedding space.

### Family-Specific Performance

| Family       | Precision | Recall | F1-score |
| ------------ | --------: | -----: | -------: |
| HSP70        |      1.00 |   1.00 |     1.00 |
| RPS3         |      1.00 |   1.00 |     1.00 |
| GAPDH        |      0.59 |   0.62 |     0.61 |
| Cytochrome C |      0.60 |   0.56 |     0.58 |

HSP70 and RPS3 were recovered almost perfectly, whereas GAPDH and Cytochrome C exhibited partial overlap in embedding space.

## Alignment-Free Baseline Comparison

To evaluate whether ESM-2 embeddings provide information beyond simple sequence composition, embeddings were compared against a classical alignment-free baseline using normalized 3-mer frequency vectors.

### Clustering Metrics

| Method                   | Cluster Purity | V-measure |
| ------------------------ | -------------: | --------: |
| 3-mer Frequency Baseline |          0.722 |     0.819 |
| ESM-2 Embeddings         |      **0.747** |     0.790 |

The two methods reveal a metric-dependent trade-off. ESM-2 achieves higher cluster purity, indicating more homogeneous family assignments within clusters. The k-mer baseline achieves a slightly higher V-measure, reflecting fragmentation into multiple locally pure subclusters.

### UMAP Comparison: 3-mer Baseline vs ESM-2

![k-mer vs ESM2](plots/kmer_vs_esm2.png)

The left panel shows UMAP projections of normalized 3-mer frequency vectors. Family structure is fragmented into multiple disconnected islands driven primarily by local sequence composition.

The right panel shows ESM-2 embeddings of the same proteins. HSP70 and RPS3 form coherent, biologically interpretable regions, while GAPDH and Cytochrome C occupy a shared continuous manifold. Unlike the k-mer baseline, the ESM representation preserves global organization across families rather than fragmenting proteins into isolated composition-based clusters.

## Biological Interpretation

The persistent overlap between GAPDH and Cytochrome C is the most interesting observation in this study.

Both proteins are ancient, highly conserved housekeeping proteins that have evolved under strong functional constraint across eukaryotes. GAPDH functions in glycolysis, while Cytochrome C serves as an electron carrier in oxidative phosphorylation. Despite their distinct biological roles, both exhibit limited sequence divergence relative to proteins such as HSP70.

ESM-2 appears to encode conservation and functional constraint as dominant signals. As a result, proteins sharing similar evolutionary histories and levels of sequence conservation can occupy neighboring regions of embedding space despite belonging to different functional families.

This highlights an important challenge in comparative genomics: distinguishing similarity arising from shared ancestry from similarity arising from shared functional constraint. The observed GAPDH–Cytochrome C overlap suggests that protein language models capture broad evolutionary patterns but may require structural information, domain context, or explicit phylogenetic priors to resolve subtle family-level distinctions.

## Embedding Outlier Detection

Within-family clustering identified sequences occupying atypical regions of embedding space.

| Family       | Total Sequences | Outliers |
| ------------ | --------------: | -------: |
| Cytochrome C |              80 |        1 |
| GAPDH        |              80 |        1 |
| HSP70        |              80 |        4 |
| RPS3         |              80 |       11 |

The elevated outlier count observed in RPS3 is noteworthy. Ribosomal protein families are known to exhibit lineage-specific duplications and paralogous variants across eukaryotes. While these outliers cannot be interpreted as confirmed paralogs without additional sequence and phylogenetic analysis, they represent promising candidates for further investigation.

## Key Findings

- ESM-2 embeddings recover biologically meaningful protein family structure without sequence alignment.
- Protein family identity can be predicted from embeddings with 79.7% accuracy, substantially exceeding a random baseline.
- Compared with a classical k-mer representation, ESM-2 embeddings produce more coherent family-level organization and higher clustering purity.
- Conserved housekeeping proteins such as GAPDH and Cytochrome C remain challenging to separate, suggesting that functional constraint can dominate embedding geometry.
- Embedding-space outliers provide candidate sequences for future investigation of divergence, duplication, or annotation inconsistencies.
